Introduction

Throughout my ownership of a 2023 Tesla Model 3 RWD, I've diligently collected an extensive dataset about the car. My goal has been to gain a comprehensive understanding of the vehicle's true efficiency and calculate the cost savings in comparison to my previous vehicle, a 2015 Volkswagen Passat 1.8T Limited Edition. I will share all the collected data and offer my personal insights, as there are several nuanced aspects to consider.

However, it's important to acknowledge that this dataset is primarily anecdotal in nature. Numerous variables, including climate, driving habits, terrain, and more, can have an influence on the efficiency of any vehicle. These variables should be kept in mind when interpreting the results.

Other Factors

As noted earlier, the efficiency of a Tesla Model 3 RWD is subject to a myriad of factors. While driving style, terrain, and climate take the forefront as primary influencers, it's essential to consider various additional factors, with some being optional choices and one being an inevitable part of the equation.

Phantom Drain
The gradual loss of battery charge when the vehicle is not in use. This can occur due to various factors such as maintaining systems like Wi-Fi, GPS, or software updates even when the car is parked and turned off.
Remote Climate Control
Involves the ability to pre-condition the vehicle's cabin temperature using the Tesla app. It allows the driver to heat or cool the cabin before getting into the car, which can affect energy consumption.
Cabin Overheat Protection
A feature that prevents the interior of the vehicle from reaching uncomfortably high temperatures when parked in hot weather. It does so by automatically cooling the cabin to a reasonable temperature.
Sentry Mode
A security feature that uses the car's external cameras to monitor the surroundings when the vehicle is parked and locked. It can record potential incidents or break-ins and deter potential thieves by displaying a warning on the cars touchscreen.

While phantom drain is an inherent aspect that can't be avoided, the remaining three factors can be viewed as premium features since their usage is optional and not mandatory.

Of course I use these features regularly, so I consider this to be a part of the vehicle's overall efficiency. Remember, this whole analysis is based of my own experience. Not every Tesla owner will use all of these features which means their efficiency will differ from mine.

Temperature plays a pivotal role in the efficiency of electric vehicles, primarily due to the battery's optimal operating conditions. April stands out as the most efficient month in my analysis, and it's no coincidence that the average high temperature during that period is an outlier. April strikes a balance between not being too hot or too cold, which contributes to higher efficiency. Conversely, during the scorching months of July and August, my efficiency dropped by approximately 21.07% compared to my overall average of 3.37 Mi/kWh.

It's worth noting that I have access to free charging sources, which slightly skews the savings aspect. However, before I had this access, I learned to maximize savings by taking advantage of Tesla Supercharging during off-peak periods. Months like May and June serve as good examples, offering a more realistic view of the potential savings achieved by choosing electric charging over traditional gasoline.

Frequently Asked Questions
How did you collect and record data for your analysis?
My data primarily derives from precise calculations using information sourced directly from the Tesla app. There are no estimations involved; all data is meticulously logged in an Excel spreadsheet with accurate formulas.
What is your driving style?
I consider my driving style to be non-aggressive, although I typically don't drive at the most efficient speeds. The majority of my Autopilot miles are accumulated during freeway driving, where I adjust my speed to match the flow of traffic, which is often above 75mph. My average Safety Score, at 97, reflects a more relaxed and defensive approach to driving.
What advice do you have for someone considering buying a Tesla Model 3 for efficiency reasons?
The Tesla Model 3 RWD undeniably boasts superior efficiency, surpassing most conventional gas vehicles and many hybrids currently on the road. Nonetheless, the primary factors often revolve around the initial cost difference and range considerations. While $20 can take you a remarkable 421 miles, it's worth noting that you might need to charge at least twice to cover that distance, whereas a typical gas vehicle can usually go that far on a single tank of gas, but can cost double the amount or more. If efficiency is a top priority, the Model 3 remains a compelling option to consider.
Can you explain more about the day-to-day practicality of owning a Tesla Model 3 in terms of efficiency?

Leaving the Cabin Overheat Protection option activated during the summer can result in significant battery drain, which varies based on the temperature and duration of use. It's more of a luxury feature rather than a necessity. Regardless, you'll likely need to run the climate control for a while to cool the cabin before entering.

In the winter, you'll find yourself warming up and defrosting the car before getting in. This process also pre-conditions the battery, adding a minor amount of battery loss.

While driving, maintaining speeds below 70 mph on the freeway is the most efficient, but it can be challenging to do so when everyone else is cruising at around 80 mph.
